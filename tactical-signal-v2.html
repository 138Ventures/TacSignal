Signal Framework — The Mathematics

This dashboard implements a tactical asset allocation (TAA) signal framework inspired by the BhFS Behavioural Finance Solutions methodology. It combines two orthogonal information sources — technical momentum and fundamental valuation — into a single composite signal using a behaviorally-determined weighting function.

1. Z-Score Normalization

Both technical and fundamental indicators are converted into z-scores by measuring how many standard deviations the current value deviates from its historical mean, using a rolling 2-year (24-month) window:

Ztech(t) = ( Xtech(t) − μtech ) / σtech
Zfunda(t) = ( Xfunda(t) − μfunda ) / σfunda
Where μ and σ are computed over a trailing 24-month window. Scores range from approximately −4 to +4.

The z-score normalization is critical: it puts all asset classes on a common scale regardless of whether the underlying indicator is a P/E ratio, a moving average crossover, or a yield spread. A score of +2 means "2 standard deviations above the 2-year mean" for any asset class.

2. Technical Indicators (Ztech)

Technical scores capture price momentum and trend-following signals. Common components include:

  • Moving Average Crossovers: Ratios of short-term (e.g. 50-day) to long-term (e.g. 200-day) moving averages. When the short MA is above the long MA, the signal is positive.
  • Price Momentum: Trailing 6-month and 12-month returns, often with the most recent month excluded to avoid short-term reversal effects (the "12-1 momentum" factor).
  • Trend Strength: Measures like the slope of a regression line through recent prices, or the fraction of time the price was above its moving average.

These components are typically combined into a single composite technical indicator, then z-scored.

3. Fundamental Indicators (Zfunda)

Fundamental scores capture valuation and macro signals. Common components include:

  • Valuation Ratios: Earnings yield (inverse P/E), book-to-price, dividend yield — all measured relative to their own history.
  • Macro Indicators: PMI, yield curve slope, credit spreads, relative GDP growth, current account balances.
  • Earnings Momentum: Revisions in consensus earnings estimates — the direction and magnitude of analyst forecast changes.

4. Behavioral Weighting Function (wtech)

This is the distinctive element of the BhFS approach. Rather than using a fixed weighting between technical and fundamental signals, the weight w_tech is determined by a behavioural objective function that adapts based on market conditions.

wtech ∈ [0, 1]
The weight on the technical score, computed via a behavioral objective function. The remaining weight (1 − w_tech) goes to fundamentals.

The intuition is that in trend-following regimes (strong momentum, low volatility), technicals should dominate — markets are "following the herd." In mean-reverting regimes (high volatility, value dislocations), fundamentals should dominate — markets are "coming to their senses." The BhFS approach uses insights from behavioral finance to determine this allocation automatically.

Key insight: In the sample data, notice how w_tech is often 0% or 100% — this is binary regime detection. Either the technical signal is deemed reliable (w=100%, fundamentals ignored) or unreliable (w=0%, pure fundamental signal). This reflects the academic finding that regime switches tend to be sharp, not gradual.

5. Composite Signal

The final composite signal is the weighted average of the two z-scores:

S = wtech × Ztech + (1 − wtech) × Zfunda

6. Signal Classification

The composite score S is converted into a discrete signal using symmetric thresholds:

Signal = OVERWEIGHT  if S > τOW  (default: +1.0)
Signal = UNDERWEIGHT if S < τUW  (default: −1.0)
Signal = NEUTRAL     otherwise
The default thresholds of ±1 correspond to ±1 standard deviation — roughly the top/bottom 16% of the historical distribution. Adjustable in the formula bar.

This means the system only generates active positions when conviction is high — the composite score must exceed a full standard deviation. The neutral zone (between −1 and +1) acts as a dead band that prevents whipsawing on marginal signals.

7. Signal Map Interpretation

The scatter plots show each asset positioned by its technical score (x-axis) and fundamental score (y-axis). Key quadrants:

  • Top-right (+/+): Both momentum and value are favorable — highest conviction overweight.
  • Bottom-left (−/−): Both unfavorable — highest conviction underweight.
  • Top-left (−/+): Cheap but falling — potential value trap or contrarian opportunity.
  • Bottom-right (+/−): Expensive but rising — momentum play, potentially vulnerable to reversal.

The arrows show the direction of travel since the previous month, revealing whether an asset is improving or deteriorating on each dimension.

8. Limitations & Caveats

This framework is a decision-support tool, not a trading system. Important limitations:

  • Z-scores assume a stationary distribution over the 2-year window — structural breaks (COVID, rate regime changes) can distort normalization.
  • The ±1σ thresholds are arbitrary and not optimized — they represent a reasonable prior, not a calibrated model.
  • The behavioral weighting function w_tech is a black box from BhFS — this dashboard lets you override it, but the proprietary logic is not replicated.
  • No transaction costs, implementation lag, or capacity constraints are considered.
  • Past z-score patterns do not guarantee future signal quality.

Technical Score — How to Compute

For each asset, you need price history to compute momentum signals. Here's a practical recipe:

raw_tech = 0.5 × (P / SMA_200 − 1) + 0.3 × mom_12_1 + 0.2 × (SMA_50 / SMA_200 − 1)

Z_tech = (raw_tech − mean(raw_tech, 24m)) / std(raw_tech, 24m)
SMA_200 = 200-day simple moving average
mom_12_1 = 12-month return excluding most recent month
SMA_50 = 50-day simple moving average
All z-scored over a trailing 24-month window of monthly observations.

w_tech — Behavioral Weight

The behavioral weighting is the proprietary BhFS component. For a practical approximation, you can use:

  • Simple regime rule: Set w_tech = 100% when the 12-month trend is strong (|mom_12| > median), and w_tech = 0% otherwise. This captures the basic idea that technicals matter more in trending markets.
  • Volatility-based: w_tech = max(0, 1 − realized_vol / threshold). High volatility → low weight on technicals.
  • Manual override: Use the What-If Scenario tab to test different w_tech assumptions across the board.

Free Data Pipeline — Practical Steps

Monthly workflow to update this dashboard:

  • Step 1: Download 3 years of monthly prices from Yahoo Finance for each asset (the ETF proxies in the table above).
  • Step 2: Compute SMA_50, SMA_200, and 12-1 momentum for each asset → technical composite → z-score.
  • Step 3: Pull valuation data from FRED (yields, spreads) and Yahoo Finance (P/E, P/B) → fundamental composite → z-score.
  • Step 4: Apply your w_tech rule (or use 50/50 as a neutral starting point).
  • Step 5: Paste the resulting CSV into this dashboard's Data Grid tab, or import as JSON.

What you can't replicate for free: The BhFS behavioral weighting function (w_tech) is proprietary. The z-score framework and signal logic are standard, but the specific behavioral regime detection that determines whether to trust technicals or fundamentals is their IP. The approximations above are reasonable but will not match their outputs exactly.
